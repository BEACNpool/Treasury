Chapter I

The Peak

Cardano was born with a war chest. A reserve of ~13.9 billion ADA was set aside at genesis — a monetary expansion pool designed to fund staking rewards and gradually fill the treasury through a carefully designed exponential decay. It was supposed to last decades.

By design, 0.3% of the remaining reserves are released each epoch (every 5 days), split between staking rewards and the treasury. 20% of every transaction fee also flows to the treasury. The system was elegant: a self-sustaining blockchain with a built-in endowment that would grow the treasury while the network matured enough to sustain itself on transaction fees alone.

By Q1 2025, the treasury had accumulated approximately 1.8 billion ADA. The reserves, while depleting by design, still held billions. The combined protocol-held ADA represented an enormous endowment — one of the largest treasuries in all of crypto.

Then the spending began.

Chapter II

The Spending Spree

Since its inception in 2020, Project Catalyst has distributed over $150 million across more than 2,100 funded proposals through 14 funding rounds and counting. It was marketed as "one of the world's largest decentralized grant programs." The question nobody asked loudly enough: what did we get for it?

The receipts tell a story the marketing never did. Many of the most prominent, most-funded projects built on Cardano are now inactive, sunset, or no longer delivering. Not struggling. Not pivoting. Gone quiet.

Chapter IV

The AI Revolution Makes This Inexcusable

Here is the truth that changes everything: the marginal cost of software is collapsing toward zero.

When Catalyst Fund 1 launched in 2020, hiring developers to build a Cardano dApp was expensive. Haskell and Plutus expertise was scarce. Tooling was immature. Paying premium rates for human-led development made sense — there was no alternative.

That world no longer exists.

AI-assisted development has advanced at exponential rates. Models that could barely autocomplete code in 2022 can now architect entire applications, write smart contracts, debug complex systems, and generate production-ready code in minutes. The cost of building software is falling so fast that by the time a Catalyst-funded project completes its milestones, the work could likely be reproduced for a fraction of the original grant.

In a world where the marginal cost of code trends toward zero, do we need to fund human-led projects at 2021 rates when they are unsustainable?

The question the treasury must answer

This isn't a theoretical concern. Consider what each Catalyst fund has allocated:

Add governance withdrawals for core infrastructure (the Pentad's ₳70M proposal for 2026, Intersect operational budgets, etc.) and we're still spending multiples of what the network earns.

Every one of these grants is priced as if AI doesn't exist. As if it still costs six figures and six months to build what an AI-augmented team can ship in weeks. We are paying 2021 prices in a 2026 world. That's not governance — it's negligence.

THE CRITICAL PARAMETER: Staking rewards must be sustained. If the reserves deplete and fee income doesn't grow, there won't be enough to fill the reward pool. This isn't about funding projects — it's about keeping the lights on. Every ADA overspent from the treasury is an ADA that could have been reserved to ensure stakers keep getting paid. If staking rewards become uncompetitive, delegators leave, security weakens, and the network spirals.

The Fee Income Reality

Transaction fees are the only truly sustainable income source. Monetary expansion from reserves is a depleting asset — it shrinks every epoch by design. The entire long-term plan was always for fee revenue to eventually replace monetary expansion as the primary funding source.

At roughly ₳5 million per year in fee revenue against ₳350 million in authorized spending, Cardano is running a deficit of approximately 98.6%. No household, no business, no nation can sustain a 98.6% deficit. The only reason Cardano hasn't collapsed is that it's drawing down a finite inheritance — the reserves.

Spending credit against future reserves is no different than a government printing money. It creates inflation, dilutes value, and pushes the consequences onto the next generation of ADA holders. Part of growing up is learning to balance a budget.

The Path Forward:
A Balanced Budget

The good news: Cardano still has time. The treasury isn't empty. The reserves are still substantial. But every epoch of overspending narrows the window. Here is what fiscal responsibility looks like.

✅ Target: Spend ≤ Replenishment Rate

Cap spending at ~₳300M (matching treasury income from monetary expansion). This is the sovereign wealth fund model the Budget Committee cited. It preserves principal and maintains optionality.

Sustainable until reserves deplete (decades)

✅ Real Target: Spend ≤ Fee Income

The ultimate goal. When Cardano can fund itself entirely from transaction fees without touching reserves or treasury principal, it becomes truly self-sustaining. This requires growing network usage dramatically — or cutting spending to match reality.

₳5M/year (current) → grow through adoption

The Principles

1. Staking rewards come first. Before a single ADA goes to a Catalyst proposal or governance withdrawal, the network must ensure that staking rewards remain competitive. This is the bedrock that secures the chain. If delegators leave because rewards crater, everything else is moot.

2. Stop paying 2021 prices. Every future proposal should be evaluated against what AI-augmented development costs today, not what it cost three years ago. If a team is requesting ₳500K for work that an AI-augmented developer can do in a weekend, the answer is no.

3. Demand ROI receipts. No more funding projects without measurable on-chain impact metrics. If a funded project can't show increased transaction volume, new users onboarded, or TVL growth, it failed — and the community deserves to know before the next round of funding.

4. Treat reserves as strength. Norway's sovereign wealth fund doesn't liquidate its holdings to fund experiments. A blockchain with a massive treasury signals confidence, stability, and long-term viability. A blockchain that hemorrhages its reserves signals desperation. Which ADA would you rather hold?

5. No more credit spending. Drawing down reserves to fund operating expenses is the blockchain equivalent of printing money. It inflates supply, creates sell pressure, and punishes long-term holders. Every ADA withdrawn is sold into the market. The Cardano Foundation's own research confirms this creates price suppression. It's time to be responsible.
